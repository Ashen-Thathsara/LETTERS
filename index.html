Project Scope

INTRODUCTION

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a neurodevelopmental condition which is characterized by challenges in communication, social interaction, and repetitive behaviors. It is called a "spectrum" disorder since it presents differently in each person, with a wide range of symptoms and varying levels of severity [1]. Some individuals with autism may have mild symptoms and be highly functional, while others might have more significant challenges that impact their daily lives. The disorder has been known under its name for more than a century. But some examples of autistic behavior had appeared in literature long before scientists named it. There is no specific cause of ASD and according to research, the cognitive and language abilities of these children can be improved by early intervention programs [2].

This research contributes to the development of knowledge for children with ASD studying in the Sri Lankan school system. Thus, to overcome the limitations mentioned above, the new contribution of "LETTERS" is,

  1. 1. Development of all three cognitive skills of reading, writing and color recognition in disabled children

  1. 2. Using both Sinhala and English mediums

  1. 3. Increasing prediction and intervention accuracy with machine learning

By focusing on early intervention, cultural adaptation and the potential integration of different methods, "Letters" has the potential to improve the cognitive abilities and overall wellbeing of children with ASD in Sri Lanka.

LITERATURE REVIEW

LITERATURE REVIEW

Looking at the current research in Sri Lanka, most of it focuses on attempts to identify children with ASD by recognizing facial expressions [3]. Using apps like Tobii Pro to capture facial expressions and analyze social interactions has been explored [4]. It seems that this eye tracking and facial expression recognition technology is adapted not to the school system but to the medical field.

Also, the “EarlySee” mobile app [5] uses facial expression recognition to assess responsiveness in children and “Volafriends” [6] recognizes emotion through facial landmark and head posture assessment, but they are often not culturally adapted and do not meet the specific needs of children in Sri Lanka.

Culturally adapted questionnaires such as the M-Chat [7] and Q-Chat are used in some areas for ASD screening, highlighting the importance of tailoring interventions to specific populations [8]. Children can improve their reading skills by playing action games in a gamified atmosphere[9]. But in order to develop skills, you need to practice daily. The study's objective is to screen individuals with neurodevelopmental problems online[10], but it is designed only for young adults. Machine learning-powered mobile app "Pubudu" focuses on reading and writing for children with neurological disabilities [11]. It offers screening and intervention for dyslexia, dyscalculia, and letter and number dysgraphia while accommodating both Sinhala and English languages. But it does not focus on identifying color disorders in children with autism.

METHODOLOGY

METHODOLOGY

This section outlines the methodology employed in evaluating the accuracy of pronunciation and assessing the effectiveness of our proposed tool.

We conducted our study on a sample comprising 150 children diagnosed with ASD and 150 typically developing children. Audio recordings of these children reading predetermined words were obtained for analysis. The collected audio files underwent preprocessing, which involved converting them into spectrogram images using the TensorFlow library. Spectrograms provide a visual representation of the frequency content of audio signals over time, facilitating the comparison of pronunciation accuracy.

The spectrogram images were further processed to extract relevant features for analysis. The TensorFlow library facilitated this feature extraction process, allowing us to convert the images into NumPy arrays, enabling numerical computations and manipulation.

To quantify the similarity between the pronunciation of the recorded words and their correct forms, we employed the Euclidean Distance metric. Euclidean Distance calculates the straight-line distance between two points in a multidimensional space, providing a measure of dissimilarity between vectors. By comparing the spectrogram images of the uttered words with their correct counterparts, we computed the Euclidean Distance to assess pronunciation accuracy

The computed similarity distances were used to evaluate the extent to which the children's voiced utterances aligned with correct pronunciation standards. Statistical analyses were performed to assess the significance of observed differences between children with ASD and typically developing children in terms of pronunciation accuracy.

Given the diversity of languages spoken by our participants, we tailored our approach to accommodate language-specific nuances. For Sinhala voice recognition, Euclidean Distance served as the similarity distance matrix, facilitating accurate assessment of pronunciation fidelity. Conversely, for English voice recognition, we utilized the cosine similarity distance matrix, recognizing its efficacy in capturing linguistic subtleties inherent to English pronunciation.
